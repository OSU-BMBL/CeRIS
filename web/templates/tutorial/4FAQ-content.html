What are a regulon and CTS-regulon and why are they important?

The term regulon was first introduced by Maas et al. in 1964, intended to name a maximal group of co-regulated genes, which may be scattered in a genome without apparent patterns in terms of their genomic locations. Based on that, for the first time, we define a CTS-regulon as a group of genes, receiving similar regulatory signals in a specific cell type, hence tend to have similar expression patterns and share conserved motifs in this cell type. A successful elucidation of CTS-regulons will substantially improve the identification of transcriptionally co-regulated gene modules, realistically allowing reliable prediction of global transcription regulation networks encoded in a specific cell type.

How do DMINDA and MEME predict regulons?

DMINDA (DNA motif identification and analyses) [link to web] is an integrated web server for DNA motif identification and analyses. The server provides a suite of cis-regulatory motif analysis functions on DNA sequences, which are important to elucidation of the mechanisms of transcriptional regulation: (i) de novo motif finding for a given set of promoter sequences along with statistical scores for the predicted motifs derived based on information extracted from a control set, (ii) scanning motif instances of a query motif in provided genomic sequences, (iii) motif comparison and clustering of identified motifs, and (iv) co-occurrence analyses of query motifs in given promoter sequences.


MEME suite [link to web]: The MEME Suite supports motif-based analysis of DNA, RNA and protein sequences. It provides motif discovery algorithms using both probabilistic and discrete models, which have complementary strengths. It also allows the discovery of motifs with arbitrary insertions and deletions (GLAM2). In addition to motif discovery, the MEME Suite provides tools for scanning sequences for matches to motifs (FIMO, MAST and GLAM2Scan), scanning for clusters of motifs (MCAST), comparing motifs to known motifs (Tomtom), finding preferred spacings between motifs (SpaMo), predicting the biological roles of motifs (GOMo), measuring the positional enrichment of sequences for known motifs (CentriMo), and analyzing ChIP-seq and other large datasets (MEME-ChIP).


Why not using the TF name to represent the regulon or motif name?

The query motif identified by IRIS3 or MEME may match to more than one target TFBS profiles in the motif databases from TOMTOM (see the following figure). Hence, it is not feasible to name the predicted motif or regulon by one TF name.

Therefore, the motif may not be unique, and two or more share the similarity may exist in one CTS-regulon.

How does IRIS3 rank regulons?

Each regulon is a set of genes that correspond to the predicted motif based on the motif scanning and prediction (see FAQ 3 above). A group of motifs predicted by DMINDA is then be clustered into subgroups composed of similar motifs using the following algorithm, which is based on a maximum spanning tree (MST) representation of the candidate motifs. In this way, it is possible that more than one motif with similarities are assigned to one regulon and the genes are then the union of all motifs. A global P-value for the entire motif instances is calculated during motif scanning by summing up the possibility of at least k instances denoted in the motif. The most significant p-value is used as the CTS-regulon p-value. The system will then sort and name regulons based on the p-values from low to high.

How is the performance of IRIS3?

To evaluate the performance of IRIS3, we compared our regulons with the result predicted by the SCENIC pipeline, using the example data. Default parameters were used for both tools.

As a result, 259 CTS-regulons, out of 399 total regulons predicted by IRIS3, were found significantly (FDR adjusted p-value <0.05) enriched to at least one pathway (KEGG_2016 database) using the EnrichR package (1.0 version), giving rise to an F-score of 0.631 (see the definition of F-score below). While, 672 regulons were identified by SCENIC following its manual. By removing low-confident-TF-annotated and less-than-two-gene regulons, 463 were left, and 184 were found significantly enrich the pathways. The F-score of SCENIC identified regulons was only 0.5465.

Meanwhile, it only takes IRIS3 21 min to predict CTS-regulons, which is about 31 times faster than SCENIC (9.5 hours).

What are the future plans?

  • Build up a database of CTS-regulon atlas in diverse cell types of human, mouse and extend to other species.

  • Integrate more sequence analysis, including quality control, alignment, mapping, and normalization in the IRIS3 pipeline to generate the normalized expression data from FASTQ.

  • An in-development tool, BRIC (biclustering-based gene regulation and cell type prediction from single-cell RNA-Seq data), will be used to replace QUBIC to predict cell types and CTS regulons simultaneously. The BRIC is integrated with (1) a left truncated mixture Gaussian model for the accurate quantification of the sample-specific gene regulatory signals and (2) a dual-core algorithm for more precise biclusters identification.

  • A more powerful in-house deep learning pipeline, DESSO, will be added in IRIS3 as a replacement of DMINDA and MEME to analyze cell-type-specific ChIP-Seq data (integrate the Cistrome database) in support of more accurate regulon prediction. DESSO (bioRxiv version) is currently under review in Genome Research
